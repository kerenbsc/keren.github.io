The MilkyWay

The Sun and all the planets around it are part of what is known as the Milky Way Galaxy. It is our home galaxy and one of the billions of galaxies in the universe. The term “milky” is derived from the galaxy's appearance from Earth ‐ a band of light in the night sky formed from stars. However, you can't see that with your naked eye. The Milky Way appears as a band as its disk-shaped structure is viewed from within. A barred spiral with a diameter ranging from 100, 000 to 1800,000 light years, our galactic home is estimated to contain between 100 and 400 billion stars.

They Milky Way has never actually been photographed. Any seemingly accurate images of it are actually illustrations or artistic representations like animations. As we are located inside the Milky Way it is impossible to photograph it as a whole.

What is it?

The Milky Way is a barred spiral galaxy that spans about 100,000 light-years in diameter and contains between 100 billion and 400 billion stars. It was formed approximately 12.5 billion years ago and continues to grow, with about 7 new stars born each year. The galaxy is primarily composed of dark matter, which makes up about 90% of its mass, and it contains a supermassive black hole at its center. The Milky Way is part of a group of galaxies known as the Local Group, which includes about 40 galaxies.

The Milky Way's appearance from Earth is characterized by a hazy band of light, which is formed by stars that are too distant to be individually distinguished. These facts provide a glimpse into the structure and characteristics of our galaxy.

What would you like to learn about?

Stars

These giant, glowing balls of hot gas, held together by gravity. Inside their cores, they produce energy by turning hydrogen into helium, which releases the light and heat we see from Earth.

Galaxies

Galaxies are huge groups of stars, planets, gas, and dust held together by gravity. They range from small systems with a few thousand stars to massive ones containing trillions, stretching across vast distances. Most galaxies have a supermassive black hole at their center.

Black Holes

With such a strong gravity that nothing, not even light can escape through, the black holds its place as a void that can't be escaped once too close. At its center is a singularity, where matter is packed incredibly tightly. Black holes also bend space and time, affecting nearby stars and gas.
